Fusion

Fusion is the nuclear process by which stars create energy. The extremely high temperatures (~ 10 million °C) and pressures (~ 265 billion times greater than Earth's atmosphere) cause hydrogen (H) atoms to fuse together when they collide, creating a Helium (He) atom and possibly also releasing highly energetic neutrons. The most energetically favourable (most likely to occur) of these reactions is the Deuterium-Tritium (D-T) reaction shown on the left. D and T fuse to produce one He atom and a highly energetic neutron. The total energy produced is 17.59MeV which exactly corresponds to the difference in masses of the D-T atoms and the fusion products according to E = Δmc2.

Magnetic Confinement Fusion

The goal of magnetic confinement fusion is to study plasmas produced in fusion devices and ultimately contain them to produce clean and effectively unlimited energy with fuel extracted from seawater. The most promising magentic confinement fusion device is called a tokamak and is essentially a toroidal device which use very strong magnets to confine plasmas. The other notable example of a magnetic fusion device is called a stellarator.

The difficulty in achieving fusion lies in the confinement of the fusion plasmas where device core temperatures can typically reach tens of millions of degrees. The tokamak is designed to confine the plasma using strong magnetic fields and minimize plasma contact with the device wall. The plasma on the other hand resists this confinement by drawing energy from gradients in temperature, density etc. which drive instabilities in the plasma. These instabilities can result in a complete breakdown of the plasma by transporting large amounts of heat, momentum and particles out of the core. The main cause of anomalous transport in fusion devices is turbulent transport.

Sheared Turbulence in MAST

My current research involves simulating ion-scale turbulence that occurs in L-mode discharges in the Mega-Ampere Spherical Tokamak (MAST). Simulations are done using the local gyrokinetic code GS2, which simulates the plasma in an idealized domain called a fluxtube, essentially a very long thin box where we assume gradients are constant.

In particular, I am interested in the effect of flow shear on turbulent structures in MAST. The images below show what unsheared and sheared turbulence looks like in MAST.
